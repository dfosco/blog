### Starting out is tough

Especially earlier in your career, it's not uncommon to run into a role that requires a skill or experience you don't have.

Not meeting all the requirements for a design job should never stop you from applying. Most candidates don't fit job descriptions to the dot, and the ones that do are not necessarily the best match.

Hiring is a hard job that comes with a lot of nuance. For most recruiters and hiring managers, finding a candidate with a **relevant match** of skills and experiences is more important than checking all of the boxes. 

Priorities also frequently change during the hiring process, depending on the pool of candidates and internal changes in the business, and what they find in a candidate might actually be a better fit for the role than the initial requirements.

They do want to check **most of the boxes**, though. So, when you're missing a few important ones, what should you do?

### Mapping job requirements

How do you position yourself as a candidate when the role you're looking at requires skills you don't have? Let's take this example and go from there:

> You just found a designer role in a company you are super interested in. They have a great product, a sustainable business model, and pay top of the range salaries. There's only one problem… the role requires **strong experience in facilitating user research**, and you never *really* focused on user research, even though you've done some of it.
> 

Most design teams these days have less designers than they need, and in most cases, the ones they do have are stretched thin. In these companies, job listings become a long list skills and requirements, with the hope that one extra designer will plug every hole and fix every problem. 

You might want to avoid these roles, but that doesn't mean they actually *require* all of that: many job descriptions are written with a “just in case” mindset, and don't necessarily map to the reality of the work. That can also mean the company has a poor grasp of the role a designer *should* have in their team.

Still, it's worth learning how to navigate these long requirements to have more options in your pockets when you need them. 

---

Regardless of the expectations for the role you're interested in, you should understand as much as possible **what they mean** when they ask for a skill. 

Out of all the qualifications required, which ones are the most important for them? Which ones are secondary? Which ones are replaceable with some other skill *you* have?

In the case of the example described above, the more you can learn about their user research and design process, the better:

- Does their design team has any blog posts or videos online? If you can't find anything design-related, see if they have something about their product management.
- Has one of their designers or researchers ever made a presentation about their experience there?
- If you find the portfolio or profile for one of their designers, what kind of work are they showing? How is their process?

You can see this search eventually gets more broad than just user research. That's ok! More than diving into one specific requirement, you want to know the rough shape of their design process, and use that to relate your experience to the role you're looking after.
