### What is framing

Framing is an exercise to come up with *professional pitch* for you as a candidate: a short description or list of statements that quickly show your experiences, achievements, and main skills. It can also be seen as your personal value proposition, or your [positioning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positioning_(marketing)) as a candidate. 

This pitch can be used to help shape your online profile, write   your CV, and even guide your responses during interviews. 

Framing is useful to show companies **how** your background is relevant, **why** your skills are a match for the role, and **what's your angle** to solve their problem.

People who are hiring in 2025 have an immense volume of candidates, and little time to spend on each candidate -- a few minutes at best, but most likely a few seconds.

Your online profile & CV are the first points of contact with them, and should convey more than just a list of previous roles and skills.

Framing is the glue that helps a recruiter or interviewer make sense of your experience: the goal is to convey **why your experience matters** for the role. 

If it does so successfully, there's a higher chance you will get a call back for a (next) interview!

### A note on writing your copy with LLMs

Like the rest of this article, this is a very personal opinion, so I will put it very bluntly: 

**LLMs are terrible at writing copy that sounds unique**. By default, their output is incredibly bland and verbose, making your text sound pretentious in a way that is not super hard to catch, unless you edit it very intentionally.

Using LLMs to bounce ideas around, get a read on your material or create an outline is not bad at all, *but*... the current reality of tech hiring is that every good role will receive hundred (if not thousands) of applications written by AI.

If your goal is to come up with a professional positioning for yourself that **sounds unique** in a way that helps you get hired, using an LLM to write your copy will give off the same vibe that everyone else has — and might just make you blend into the pile.

Ultimately, use LLMs to the extent that you find them useful: if they speed you up and you like the output, then go for it! But keep this advice in mind, and at least **edit heavily** before you're done.

### To move forward, start by looking back

To frame your experience, looking back at your personal and professional history is the first step: more than just listing past roles and formal education, take note of volunteering projects, artistic pursuits, or informal training.

<aside>
📌 Take 5-10 minutes to write down a timeline of your personal and professional history, taking note of interesting life experiences that had a meaningful impact on your career.

Doing this is specially fun on paper, since you can take space, draw and annotate the timeline any way you want. But really, anything works: a google doc, figjam board, etc.

Once you're done, try presenting your timeline to a friend or colleague. It can be a good way to get feedback on the parts they find especially relevant, and their thoughts on your presentation in general.

I learned this technique on [a workshop by Abby Covert](https://pt.slideshare.net/AbbyCovert/everyone-needs-a-portfolio-a-workshop) a long time ago. Ceck out her deck for more details and examples of the timeline!
</aside>

When writing these down, it's important to keep an open mind, and really consider the experiences that informed your current path. Not every aspect of your story will be relevant in your CV or portfolio, but some can definitely be a part of your professional presentation.

This is especially relevant for people that did not have a formal design education or are coming into a design career from a different industry: our knowledge and expertise shape our form of thinking in ways we don't fully realize. In the right settings, your unique experiences will be an advantage, and something you can use to highlight your profile. 

Candidates with varied backgrounds frequently call my attention when I'm interviewing. It's especially interesting when a candidate can explain how their background specifically contributes to the way they approach design. 

For me, seeing how a person thinks is one of the most rewarding aspects of being an interviewer for design roles. It's a privilege to see people show a part of their story, and their approach as professional designers.

### Preparing your pitch

With your timeline in hands, it's now time to list out all the parts that might show up in your professional presentation:

- Your most recent jobs or roles
- Your most recent projects
- Your recent education, formal or otherwise
- Your areas of interest or skills (this could be anything from user research, to learning to speak a new language)

Out of this list and your timeline, which parts most strongly inform your work as a designer today? Which ones are most important to you, and why? Mark a few that you consider highlights, and that paint a good picture of who you are as a professional today. 

Try writing a sentence or two expanding each highlight: doing so can help you better present these experiences when the time comes.

---

To condense some of the information you listed so far, you'll write a short pitch of yourself as a professional. A successful pitch will tell about your current role and recent experience, but most importantly, it will **display your qualities and advantages** as a designer.

This pitch can take many forms, but it is essentially an answer to the question: **”tell me about your experience as a designer**”. This is a question you will hear in almost every single interview, in one way or another. 

Your pitch will provide you with the structure to respond to it confidently, and serve as a framework to highlight the same qualities across other presentations and interviews.

Here's an updated version of my professional pitch, similar to the ones I used when interviewing in the last couple of years:

> I'm a product designer building platforms and systems for the web in last 12 years. I am currently living in The Netherlands, and working at GitHub.
> 
> Right now I'm designing onboarding and learning experiences at GitHub, supporting developers to start adopting GitHub Copilot and finding new ways it can help them do more — regardless if they are experienced developers or just learning how to code.
>
> Before GitHub, I lead design on the Miro developer platform, re-designed the deployments product for GitLab, and built internal tools at Booking.com
> 
> I'm interested in working with complex technical domains that enable developers to do more and unlock product growth.

Two things you can notice about this pitch:

This pitch is very modular, and I can replace pieces of it depending on which kind of role I would be applying for. 

It also leverages my previous experience on developer tools, which is very specific to my career path, and a beat I've worked on for the last few years.

However, let's say I was applying to a role where developer experience is not a huge plus -- instead, having cross-platform design experience is more important. 

I'm not super experienced with mobile app design, but I've done my fair share of responsive design, so this is how I would frame my experience instead:

> I'm a designer designing products for web and mobile in the last 12 years. I am currently living in The Netherlands, and working at GitHub.
>
> Right now I'm designing onboarding and learning experiences at GitHub across platforms, ensuring our developers have a world-class experience no matter which device they're in. Before, I designed for an array of products such as Miro, GitLab and Booking.com.
> 
> In my time at Booking.com, I lead design for their new Rewards feature, working on interfaces across all of their consumer platforms: web, mobile, native apps — and even emails.

Everything listed above is true of course. I'm just zooming-in on past projects that better match what I read in the job description. 

Notably, this cross-platform project at Booking.com happened way back in 2018 and I'm really not super confident with my experience designing native apps today -- but if you need that job, you need that job. 

Once you're in the room, how you present your projects and process tends to matter more than any specific set of design skills, and although native app design can be quite unique, it's possible other qualities could make up for any shortcomings.

If we were to generalize the structure of the examples above, here's how we could do it:

> Current job title and what I do in general. Where I'm working at, and which city I’m based on.
>
> Current role in more detail / what's my team / what I focus on. Possibly an overview of projects or my process, overall goals of the team / product.
> 
> Similarly for previous role: who I collaborated with, some of my design process, goals and/or accomplishments.
> 
> List relevant skills for this role and how I displayed them in previous project / Additional sentence to highlight what you're interested in

If it looks a bit long, that's because *it is*. Being concise is important when interviewing, but I try to keep my pitch as complete as possible, and edit / scale it down depending on the audience. 

You can keep this complete version as a “source”, and create shorter versions from it, depending on the role you're applying to: maybe this role doesn't have a lot to do with marketing, so you could drop the reference to your previous brand designer role; perhaps designers don't code on this other role, so you don't need to mention that at all.

Personally, I like keeping my pitch fresh in my mind (and sometimes tucked away in my screen) while I interview, and adjust what I'm responding according to the situation. Sometimes I'll drop a sentence or two to make it swifter, sometimes I'll run a bit longer if there's time.

Make sure to find the approach that works for you: be it a pitch that you rehearse and repeat every time, or one that you adjust on the fly, it's *your* story you're presenting, so the format should fit your **style.

### Defining your angle

On top of having a pitch, defining an “angle” is a strategy I adopted to help differentiate my CV and presentation when applying for a role.

An angle is similar to a pitch, but more compact: a **single sentence** where you show what you bring to the table.

It's all about finding that *one* sentence that can help uniquely describe why your experience is great for this role.

Below are a few examples I used in the past:

> I’m passionate about solving real problems for real people: understanding their needs through research, collaborating with my team to define a vision, and designing systems that enable people to do more.

> I'm interested in owning an entire technical domain and solving hard problems for professional users

> I'm driven by design work that combines complex technical domains with understanding the reality of users, building products that allow them to get stuff done and move on with their lives.

I've used some of these examples as part of my pitch, others were used in cover letters or application questions, and one of them is part of my current LinkedIn description.

You can see they are fairly specific in language:

- “…design work that combines **complex technical domains** …”
- “…solving hard problems for **professional users**”
- “…**collaborating with my team** to define a vision, and **designing systems** that enable people to do more”

This was meant to tailor the framing to the specific roles I was applying for at the time, and to avoid phrases that were too generic and might dilute the impact of the message:

- “I design to solve problems”
- “I focus on having a user-centered design process”
- “I'm a multi-disciplinary designer”

There's nothing inherently wrong with the sentences and terms above. They describe qualities a good product designer will have, but the thing is — these are generic enough to describe **most** good product designers. 

To avoid blending in, try to find **the specific kind of work** you're excited about in a company, or the specific way you applied your design process in the past, and make that a part of your angle.

It might seem like it's not the case, but enthusiasm can go a long way when finding differentiators between candidates.

Frequently when discussing candidates, hiring managers will informally use short-hand to refer to people they've talked to like “that designer who was really good with motion” or “that one that was great at research”.

### Closing notes

While the ”tell me about your experience as a designer” question might be repeated  multiple times during a hiring process, **it is most important on the initial steps**.
 
As you advance and the interviewing team gets to know you, they will be more interested in the specifics of your work and projects than your overall trajectory.

Every interviewer talking to you (hopefully) has also looked at your CV, LinkedIn and portfolio. When they give you the chance to tell your story, list your past roles and projects of course, but remember that's also stuff they can read elsewhere. 

This is the moment to sell them on your framing, your story, and your value as a candidate. That's why the pitch is so important, to help you structure these arguments to land a great impression.

More than just trying to know who you are as a designer, they're looking for information that confirms that you're the right designer for that role.

**But seriously, be concise**

Before we switch subjects: during interviews, **it's essential to be mindful of time, and avoid** **running too long when presenting**. 

It's not rare to see candidates get caught up in their story and end up talking straight about themselves for 15 minutes or more — which for a 30 minutes interview is a lot. 

This is not a hard and fast rule, but considering a 30 minute interview, when asked to go over your professional experience, your answer should probably be longer than 2 minutes, and shorter than 10. 

Sometimes the interviewer will tell you to present yourself in 5 or 10 minutes, in which case you know what they're expecting.

This is where the timing becomes even more important, though: try your best to stay within the time they gave you. Consider that when they give you a time window, it's because they're likely short on time.

If you don't, it's likely the interviewer will cut you off “in the interest of time”, or worse — they won't, and you'll lose time that could be used to cover other questions.

Candidates who don't follow time constraints can give the impression they are unable to be concise and stay on point, and even worse, they can be seen as lacking respect of others' time.
